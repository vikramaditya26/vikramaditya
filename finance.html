1. Understanding Money and Personal Finance

A short contrarian story that rewires how you think about money.

Once a trader and a monk lived in the same town. The trader bragged of his wealth — clothes, food, status. The monk had a bowl, a roof, and a calm face. A storm came; markets froze; the trader had supply chains blocked and fortunes undone. The monk kept walking, calm, because he had no obligations. The lesson: wealth is not only the amount in your bank; it's the liabilities you carry and the options you have.

Money is a tool that buys options. The fewer obligations you have, the greater your optionality.

Beginner — a few practical truths

  • Record one month of cash flow. Write down your net income and every outgoing rupee. If you don't record, you don't know.
  • Pay yourself first. Automate transferring a fixed amount to savings/investments on payday.
  • Emergency fund > fancy investments. If you have a medical emergency and no buffer, all clever investments mean nothing.
Example: Sanjay earns ₹45,000/month. Last month he spent ₹47,000 because of festival shopping and a loan EMI. That mismatch leads to borrowing and stress. The fix: cut wants by ₹3,000 and automate ₹5,000 to a savings account each month.

Intermediate — the vocabulary of risk

There are three useful classifications of risk for a person:

  1. Idiosyncratic risk: related to you — health, career. Can be diversified by insurance and skills.
  2. Systemic risk: market-wide shakes — recession, currency crisis. Prepare with asset allocation and cash buffer.
  3. Liquidity risk: inability to convert assets quickly without loss. Keep some truly liquid funds.

Advanced — antifragility & optionality

A fragile person breaks under stress (over-leveraged), a robust person resists stress (insured, diversified), and an antifragile person gains from stress (small optional bets that pay off massively). In personal finance, aim for robustness and small, repeated optionality: keep a safe base, then make many small asymmetric bets (e.g., skill investments, small start-ups, long-term equity bets).

If you learn only one concept from this section: avoid ruin. If you avoid permanent ruin, you keep the chance to benefit from time and randomness.

2. Budgeting and Saving

Budgeting is designing a life that can tolerate shocks. This is a field guide — not theory.

There are two types of people: those who sleep soundly and those who don't. The difference is often a buffer of cash and habits. Budgeting is not "restriction"; it's establishing a margin for error. Margin means freedom.

Beginner — the three-jar experiment

Start with three jars (virtual or real): Essentials, Savings, Wants. Put a fixed percent into Savings the moment you receive income. Try a pragmatic rule: 

50% → Essentials (rent, food, bills)
30% → Wants (fun, non-essential)
20% → Savings / Investments

Adjust the numbers based on where you live and how much you owe. If you carry debt with high interest, reduce wants and attack the debt.

Worked example: Riya earns ₹60,000. She automates: ₹12,000 → SIP (20%) ₹30,000 → Essentials (50%) ₹18,000 → Wants (30%)

The emergency fund (beginner → intermediate)

Build a fund equal to 6 months of essential expenses (12 months if freelance/irregular income). Where to keep it?

  • Savings account: instant access, low return.
  • Liquid mutual fund: slightly higher return, fairly liquid (T+1/T+0 depending on fund & AMC).
  • Bank sweep-in FD: gives slightly higher interest and liquidity.

Intermediate — mental models to automate saving

Automation is the simplest trick. Use standing instructions, auto-debits for SIPs, or payroll deduction to fund PPF/SIP before you see the money.

Commitment device: Set a "no-spend" day each month or use an app that blocks shopping sites for the first 7 days after salary.

Advanced — buckets and allocation

Think in buckets:

  1. Buffer bucket: emergency fund — cash/liquid.
  2. Goal bucket: near-term goals (1–3 years) — FDs, debt funds.
  3. Growth bucket: long-term goals — equity (SIP / index funds), PPF for tax-efficient, debt-equity mix for stability.

Worked arithmetic — how much to save monthly

Suppose you want ₹10 lakh in 10 years. If you can achieve 8% annual return via SIPs, how much monthly?

Use the SIP formula or try the calculator later on this page. Rough back-of-envelope: For moderate returns, ₹5-8k per month often compounds into lakhs over decades. Discipline matters more than exact figure early on.

Tools & Indian tips:

  • Use UPI standing instructions and bank standing instructions (SIP mandates) — many AMCs allow UPI SIPs now.
  • For goal-tracking, use RBI/SEBI/AMFI resources or apps that only read data (avoid apps asking for login credentials).

3. Basics of Investing

From the boring arithmetic of compounding to the psychology that makes people buy high and sell low.

Investing is the opposite of instant gratification. The tree that yields fruit must be planted and watered. Most people plant a bonsai and expect mangoes. Before you invest, ask: what is my time horizon? The answer will determine the correct instrument.

Instruments (India-focused primer)

Low-risk / Guaranteed

  • Bank Savings Account (liquid, low return)
  • Fixed Deposits (short/medium term, secure)
  • Public Provident Fund (PPF) — long-term, tax benefits

Market-based / Growth

  • Mutual Funds — equity, debt, hybrid
  • Index Funds & ETFs — low-fee, broad market exposure
  • Stocks — higher risk, requires knowledge

Compound interest — step-by-step calculation

The formula: Future Value = P × (1 + r)^n. Let's walk it with numbers — slowly and carefully, because everyone talks compounding but very few compute it properly.

Example A — Lump-sum

Invest ₹100,000 today at 8% annually for 10 years.

  1. r = 0.08 → 1 + r = 1.08
  2. 1.08^10 = 2.158925 (approx)
  3. Future Value = 100,000 × 2.158925 ≈ ₹215,892

Example B — SIP (monthly)

Invest ₹5,000/month for 20 years at an assumed 12% annual return (monthly r = 1%).

  1. Monthly r = 0.12 / 12 = 0.01
  2. n = 20 × 12 = 240 months
  3. FV = P × [((1 + r)^n − 1)/r] × (1 + r)
  4. Compute: (1.01)^240 ≈ 10.957 → ((10.957 − 1)/0.01) = 995.7 → ×1.01 ≈ 1005.657
  5. FV ≈ 5,000 × 1005.657 ≈ ₹4,995,739 ≈ ₹50 lakh
Translation: Discipline + monthly habit + time = big difference. You do not need to pick perfect stocks; you need to keep investing while life goes on.

Where people commonly fail (psychology)

  • Short-termism: checking the portfolio every day is a form of punishment.
  • Herding: buying after a run-up because everyone is cheering.
  • High fees: paying for "active" where "passive" would do.

Practical starter steps — a sequence

  1. Build 6–12 months emergency fund.
  2. Buy adequate health insurance and term plan if you have dependents.
  3. Start a monthly SIP in a low-cost Nifty/Sensex index fund (choose a reliable AMC or ETF).
  4. After a year, learn about select individual stocks; keep them as a small portion (10–20%) only.

Where to start in India — recommended first vehicles

  • Index mutual funds / ETFs: Nifty 50 or Sensex tracking funds (low-cost).
  • PPF: For tax-efficient long-term, especially if you seek safety + returns.
  • SIP in equity funds: For disciplined, long-term investing.
